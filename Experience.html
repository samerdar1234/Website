JOURNi

Role: Coding Education Intern

Duration: 8-week summer internship (2-week Pre-Program Preparation, 6-week Student Learning Experience)

Overview: JOURNi is a non-profit organization that strives to kickstart local economies in underserved, urban areas. Its mission includes equipping youth with basic technological and entrepreneurial skills that they can build on and apply to help themselves and their communities.

Goal: Develop and execute a student program focused on website skills enablement.

  1. Pre-program preparation - Create an academic curriculum, schedule, and visual tools necessary to teach the following technical skills:
    • Typing
    • HTML
    • CSS
    • Bootstrap
    • Github
    • Gitpod
    • Trello
  2. Student learning experience - Teach the students to design and create a personal website, to produce a website to creatively tackle a problem in their community, and to create a fully functional website for a local business.

Pre-Program Preparation (2-weeks)

Constructing the materials needed to enable a positive student learning experience.

Self-Directed Learning

To ensure I was prepared for this internship, I was provided an overview of HTML, CSS, etc. I taught myself to use the requisite tools and created a reference website overnight.


Teaching Materials Design and Development

Before diving into the creation of tools to support the academic process, the other coding education interns and I reviewed existing Powerpoint decks from previous years as the rudimentary base for our teaching materials. Through this diagramming exercise, we each identified many areas for improvement. My team then worked asynchronously to make changes to the slide deck but reconvened to reevaluate those individual improvements and determine the most effective organization to ensure a positive learning environment from both a teaching and student perspective. Student Learning Experience (6-weeks)

Executing a positive student learning experience.

Academic Enablement

I assisted the professors within the virtual classroom across all aspects of the student learning experience

  • I helped teach different aspects of HTML, CSS, and Design Thinking to all the students
  • Tutoring: I worked one-on-one with students that fell behind due to missed class sessions and I'd catch them up on items missed or in areas where they struggled
  • Mentoring: I would troubleshoot with individual students on their personal projects. I also coached the students in how to give a good presentation within a team.
  • Creative Teaching: I devised projects the students could work on to practice their HTML and CSS skills.
  • Role Modeling: I worked on the same project the students work on so that I could model either different elements to include in their own projects or give them ideas on how to use the elements to make their project more interesting
  • Team Leadership: Led the other Coding Education Interns, coordinating who would present and what in order to provide a united and organized experience for the students
  • Project Management: For the student solution tackling a problem in their community and for the small business websites, I defined time-sensitive deliverables and followed up with the students to ensure they were on schedule or adjust as needed to make sure progress was made to their goals.
  • I was the liaison between the small business owner and the student developers creating the site.

What I Learned

There is always more to learn regardless of the field, however, through working with this program, I have improved upon my existing technical skills as well as soft skills. Below is a more detailed explanation of the skills I developed throughout this program.

  • I furthered my skills in HTML, CSS, Bootstrap, and Github through teaching it to others
  • Communication: By juxtaposing this experience with previous technological mentorship roles I've had, I discovered that regardless of the community, interest, and previous tech experience, if a person is given a set of expectations, they can learn and exceed those expectations if they are treated with respect and spoken to as a capable peer
  • Collaboration: I had to work heavily with the other interns and instructors to develop the course. Then, throughout the course, the instructing team also had to constantly work together to present a cohesive, comprehensible program to the students. In addition to working with the instructing team, I also took on a leadership role within a team of students to produce an attractive, useful product for the small business website.
  • Problem Solving: Within the program, the majority of my day-to-day operations was finding and fixing bugs in student code. Due to the challenges of teaching in a virtual environment, I had to constantly work with the other interns and the instructors to solve technological issues for the students. Many of the students used borrowed computers provided by JOURNi and had difficulty learning how to use the computers and share their screens to show us their code. I quickly learned how to talk the students through the steps of sharing their code, but if that failed, I had to adapt and be able to talk them through a problem based on their description.
  • Time Management: I helped establish a schedule for the 6-week student program and adjusted the time frame based on student progress.

University of Michigan

Role: Instructional Aide for Elementary Programming Concepts

Duration: 2 1/2 Semesters (1/2 completed, 2 more semesters of teaching anticipated)

Overview: The University of Michigan's Computer Science (CS) curriculum includes several core classes that are foundational to a degree in CS. Due to the popularity of the field and the correspondingly high number of students that aspire to major in the field, the core classes are designed to rigorously challenge student candidates with the goal of identifying the students best equipped to succeed.

Goal: To aid in identifying the best candidates, the computer science department provides instructional assistance for all students enabling them an opportunity to secure an effective grasp on material fundamental to the CS major.



Teaching assistant

I provided technical support for a class that focused on elementary programming concepts; a core requirement within the major. Students must successfully pass this class as a prerequisite for declaring a CS major. My work focused on three main areas:

  • Virtually reviewing class material with a group of 15-30 students in a discussion section.
  • One-on-one help with coding in both C++ and Python extended to help students "debug" /identify problems with the code, and correct the problem.
  • Tailored explanation of why the issue occurred to the student's understanding level. This was critical to reinforcing the information and ensuring a fundamental understanding and knowledge base.

What I Learned

By working with students in a larger discussion group as well as on a more personal level, I have found that the students tend to have a different objective than the instructors for what the students should be getting from the course. The instructors want the students to learn and understand as much as possible and the students tend to also want to learn but ultimately want to perform well in the class. As a teaching assistant, I learned how to balance those objectives so each student would get a combination of thoroughly learning the material and learning ways to approach problems they may see on an exam.

I also learned how to more effectively translate a technical problem into a nonntechnical explanation to suit students of all skill levels.
